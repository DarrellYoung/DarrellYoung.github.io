Professional Summary

I am a defense systems engineer and researcher whose career-long focus has been making mission-critical sensing and communication systems work reliably in contested, degraded, denied, intermittent, and limited-bandwidth (DDIL) environments. Two threads run through thirty-five years of work:

Thread 1 — Video Interpretability Science for ISR. I served as the nation's recognized subject matter expert for video interpretability for over a decade, co-authoring the Video-NIIRS RP 0901 standard and the Motion Imagery Quality Equation (MIQE). This work established the quantitative science linking sensor parameters, compression artifacts, and transmission degradation to analyst decision-making performance — enabling requirements-based acquisition of surveillance systems across DoD and the Intelligence Community.

Thread 2 — Autonomous Spectrum Access and Resilient Communications. I co-invented core technology for interference avoidance in autonomous dynamic spectrum access (U.S. Patent 8,289,907 — 196 citations, among the most referenced patents in cognitive radio). From early work on blind signal geolocation at Asilomar in 1990 through modern cognitive radio and decentralized control architectures, this thread addresses how communications systems autonomously find, negotiate, and exploit available spectrum under contested electromagnetic conditions.

The connection: DDIL conditions are the common adversary. Video interpretability degrades when communication links fail — compression artifacts increase, metadata is lost, and analyst performance drops. Cognitive radio and dynamic spectrum access provide the resilient communication channels that preserve interpretability by finding open spectrum with better characteristics. My SPIE C8-ISR paper at DCS 2026 and three MILCOM 2026 papers make this connection explicit, unifying the two threads into a single cross-echelon ISR framework.

Two Major Technical Contributions

1. National Leadership in Video Interpretability Science and Standards

For over a decade I led the scientific foundation for how the U.S. Intelligence Community measures and predicts the quality and usability of motion imagery from airborne and space-based surveillance platforms. Key achievements include:

  • Video-NIIRS RP 0901 — Co-authored the reference publication that formalized the Video National Imagery Interpretability Rating Scale, now the operational standard across DoD and the IC for specifying sensor performance requirements (10 citations as a standard document).
  • Motion Imagery Quality Equation (MIQE) — Co-developed the predictive model linking sensor parameters, compression artifacts, and transmission conditions to interpretability ratings.
  • Cognitive modeling for interpretability — Pioneered models predicting how human analysts perceive and extract intelligence from degraded video under operational conditions.
  • Video resilience decision frameworks — Created decision frameworks for determining when and how to engage video resilience mechanisms under bandwidth-constrained and contested communication conditions.
  • U.S. Patent 9,858,656 — Video Interpretability and Quality Estimation (7 citations).

Key publications: Video NIIRS Criteria Survey Results (21 cites), Loss of Interpretability Due to Compression Effects (12 cites), Low-Slant Angle Factors (9 cites), Motion Imagery Metadata Standards (7 cites), plus 10+ SPIE publications on video quality and interpretability. SPIE Airborne ISR conference co-chair, 2011–2016.

2. Autonomous Spectrum Access and Resilient Communications for Contested Environments

I have made foundational contributions to autonomous dynamic spectrum access — technology that enables communications systems to intelligently find, negotiate, and use available radio spectrum without centralized coordination. Key achievements include:

  • U.S. Patent 8,289,907 — Interference Avoidance for Autonomous Dynamic Spectrum Access (196 citations, among the most cited patents in cognitive radio). This patent family established methods for communications systems to autonomously detect, characterize, and avoid interference in real-time.
  • U.S. Patent 8,155,127 — Autonomous Dynamic Spectrum Access (22 citations).
  • Blind Capture and Geolocation — At the 1990 Asilomar Conference, co-authored foundational work on blind capture and geolocation of spatially self-coherent waveforms using multiplatform SCORE (22 citations).
  • Decentralized Control Architectures — Invented decentralized control for beam-generating devices (U.S. Patent 12,141,668) and adaptive importance encoding (U.S. Patent 11,984,964).
  • GPS-Denied Navigation — Invented navigational error correction using signals of opportunity (U.S. Patent 7,477,986).

The dynamic spectrum access patent family (196 + 22 citations) is among the most referenced intellectual property in cognitive radio — a field that has reshaped both military communications doctrine and commercial spectrum policy, including the CBRS framework and FCC spectrum sharing rules.

The Unifying Thread: DDIL as the Common Adversary

The two contributions above converge at the operational reality of DDIL. When spectrum access is degraded, communication links degrade, and the video imagery that analysts rely on for time-critical decisions loses interpretability. Cognitive radio restores that link by autonomously finding channels with better characteristics. My current and forthcoming work makes this connection explicit through a cross-echelon C8-ISR framework that integrates spectrum-aware communications with interpretability-preserving data delivery.

Work in Progress

The following research directions are under active development. Descriptions reflect problem framing and research scope; solution architectures are subject to pending IP filings.

Trustworthy Autonomous Control for Next-Generation RAN and Spectrum Systems

AI and reinforcement learning are increasingly proposed for managing 5G/O-RAN and future 6G radio access networks, but long-horizon autonomous control introduces compounding error, oscillation, and safety risks. I am investigating architectures that bound autonomous RAN control decisions within deterministic safety envelopes defined by telecom protocol constraints, emission limits, and interference budgets — enabling stable, auditable, long-horizon autonomous operation in contested and shared spectrum environments. This work extends my earlier contributions in cognitive radio and dynamic spectrum access into the era of AI-managed communications infrastructure.

Governance-Aware AI Control Planes for Shared Spectrum

SAS/CBRS solved significant technical coordination challenges for shared spectrum. What remains underdeveloped is the economic and governance legitimacy layer for future AI-managed spectrum coexistence. I am exploring programmable governance and policy automation frameworks that define auditable policy envelopes above SAS-like coordination systems — reducing operator risk, supporting regulatory transparency, and enabling trustworthy multi-stakeholder coexistence in 6G spectrum environments. This work connects to my cognitive radio heritage by extending dynamic spectrum access into auditable multi-stakeholder governance.

Cross-Echelon C8-ISR Under DDIL: Connecting Spectrum Cognition to Information Quality

My SPIE DCS 2026 and MILCOM 2026 papers address the operational gap between spectrum-aware communications and ISR information quality. When communication links degrade under DDIL conditions, video interpretability drops — compression artifacts increase, metadata is lost, and analyst decision performance suffers. This work develops a cross-echelon framework that integrates spectrum-aware channel selection with interpretability-preserving data delivery, formally connecting my Video-NIIRS and cognitive radio contributions into a unified mission systems architecture.

Quantum Zero-Knowledge Proof Architectures

In collaboration with the SMU Quantum Information Research Group, I am investigating quantum extensions to zero-knowledge proof systems for policy-bounded execution and trusted operational workflows. Forthcoming presentation at the 2026 Dallas Circuits and Systems conference.

Short Academic Bio

Dr. Darrell L. Young, P.E. is a Research Professor at the Southern Methodist University Darwin Deason Institute for Cyber Security (SMU-DDI) and an Adjunct Professor in the SMU Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering. He is a former Raytheon Principal Technical Fellow with a 38-year career in defense systems engineering.

His two major technical contributions are in video interpretability science for ISR — including co-authorship of the Video-NIIRS RP 0901 standard and the Motion Imagery Quality Equation — and in autonomous spectrum access and resilient communications — anchored by a patent family (U.S. 8,289,907; 196 citations) that is among the most referenced intellectual property in cognitive radio. His work addresses the common challenge of maintaining mission system performance under DDIL (denied, degraded, intermittent, and limited-bandwidth) conditions, connecting ISR interpretability with spectrum-aware communications.

He is an IEEE Senior Member (36 years), a member of the IEEE Aerospace and Electronic Systems Society (21 years), IEEE Circuits and Systems Society, and IEEE Communications Society, and a Life Member of SPIE and USGIF.
